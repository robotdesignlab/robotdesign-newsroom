In a move that expands collaborative robotics into heavy payload applications, Universal Robots (UR) today announced a partnership with German automation specialist KUKA to develop the UR30, a cobot capable of handling up to 30 kilograms. The UR30 integrates UR's intuitive programming interface with KUKA's heavy-duty joint modules, enabling safe human-robot collaboration in tasks such as automotive powertrain assembly and large-part machining.

The key technical advancement is the UR30's torque-controlled joints, which use six-axis force/torque sensors to detect collisions within 50 milliseconds and stop motion before injury occurs, while maintaining a repeatability of ±0.03 mm. This addresses a critical gap in the cobot market, where payloads above 20 kg were previously restricted to industrial robots requiring safety cages.

According to UR's Chief Product Officer, Anders Billesø, the UR30 targets Tier 1 automotive suppliers that need to lift engine blocks, transmission cases, and battery modules. The cobot operates at speeds up to 1.5 m/s with a reach of 1,400 mm, and its power and force limiting (PFL) software can dynamically adjust torque based on the proximity of human workers detected by an integrated 3D vision camera. The system also includes a new 'soft motion' mode that reduces inertia by 40% compared to traditional robot arms, allowing end-of-arm tools to be changed without re-training the kinematics.

KUKA contributed a hollow-shaft design that routes power and data cables internally, protecting them from snagging in high-density production lines. The UR30's wrist supports up to 50 Nm of torque, making it suitable for screwdriving and riveting operations. The robot is priced at approximately $65,000, with availability starting Q3 2024.

Industry analysts note that the UR30 could displace smaller industrial robots in applications where floor space and worker safety are priorities. The cobot's embedded AI safety controller uses machine learning to predict human motion based on 200 Hz camera data, reducing unnecessary stops by 35% in trials at BMW's Dingolfing plant. However, the UR30 still requires a risk assessment for each installation, as its 30 kg payload exceeds the ISO 10218-1 limit for collaborative robots without additional safety measures.

The launch comes as the global cobot market is projected to reach $12.3 billion by 2026 (via Interact Analysis), with heavy payload models growing at 22% CAGR. Universal Robots plans to demonstrate the UR30 at the upcoming Automatica trade fair in Munich, where live assembly of a welding jig will be performed.
