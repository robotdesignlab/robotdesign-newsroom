Walden Robotics today emerged from stealth with $300 million in funding, launching at a $1.1 billion valuation to build general-purpose robots that continuously learn while working. The company aims to combine large behavior models with real-world operations, allowing robots to take on difficult-to-automate tasks so human experts can focus on problem-solving and judgment.

Founded by Dr. Russ Tedrake, a professor at MIT and former senior vice president of large behavior models at Toyota Research Institute (TRI), Walden Robotics was spun out of TRI in January. Tedrake noted that "core advances in physical AI, and all of the excitement and attention surrounding it, has made disruptive change possible," but emphasized that providing real value requires understanding how manufacturing is done today. The startup's co-founders include experts in robotics and AI from Stanford and Amazon.

> "Core advances in physical AI, and all of the excitement and attention surrounding it, has made disruptive change possible," stated Dr. Russ Tedrake, co-founder and CEO of Walden. "But providing real value to customers and building a robust and scalable business requires a deep understanding and respect for how manufacturing is done today. The best way to make fast and positive progress is by working closely together with the real experts."

The Cambridge, Mass.-based company said its robots are designed to let people "craft with purpose," reflecting a principle that transformative technology should expand human potential. Tedrake highlighted the team's strong relationship with Toyota and alignment on the mission of amplifying, not replacing people.
