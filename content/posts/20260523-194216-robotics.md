IL, a South Korean robotics company, has secured an initial contract worth 3 billion won (approximately $2.3 million) for its humanoid robot 'ILBOT', marking a significant step toward commercialization. The contract, announced on [date], involves the supply of ILBOT units to an unnamed customer, likely in the manufacturing or service sectors, for deployment in tasks requiring human-like dexterity and mobility.

ILBOT is a full-sized humanoid robot designed for industrial applications, featuring advanced bipedal locomotion, upper-body manipulation, and autonomous navigation capabilities. The robot stands approximately 1.6 meters tall and weighs 70 kg, with a payload capacity of 5 kg per arm. Its key hardware includes 40 degrees of freedom (DoF) across joints, driven by high-torque actuators and harmonic drives for smooth, precise motion. Onboard cameras, LiDAR, and IMU sensors enable real-time environment mapping and obstacle avoidance, while a multi-core processor handles motion planning and AI inference locally.

The contract is described as 'initial', suggesting that IL is targeting a phased rollout. ILBOT's core technology is based on IL's proprietary control algorithms and hardware design, which the company has been developing since founding in [year]. Unlike many humanoid robots that rely on off-the-shelf components, IL claims its in-house actuators and control systems reduce cost and improve reliability for industrial settings.

The 3 billion won deal is a milestone for the South Korean humanoid robot industry, which has seen increasing investment from government and private sectors. IL plans to deliver the first units within six months, with additional contracts expected as the technology matures. The company aims to address labor shortages in manufacturing, logistics, and healthcare, particularly in tasks like assembly, picking, and patient assistance.

This contract comes amid a global push for humanoid robots, with companies like Tesla, Boston Dynamics, and Figure racing to bring products to market. ILBOT's advantage lies in its focus on practical, affordable hardware for Asian manufacturing ecosystems, where labor costs and aging populations drive demand for automation. IL expects to ramp up production capacity to 50 units per year initially, targeting a price point below $50,000 per robot to compete with human wages over a three-year payback period.

Technical details: ILBOT uses a modular joint design with integrated encoder, brake, and clutch for safety, supporting backdrivability for collaborative operation. Its battery provides 2 hours of continuous operation, with hot-swappable packs for 24/7 use. The control stack includes a custom RTOS and ROS 2 compatibility for software development.

IL's long-term roadmap includes adding hand dexterity with tactile sensors and expanding to outdoor navigation. The company plans to announce its next funding round later this year to scale production.
